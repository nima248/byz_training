Aural Foundations for Byzantine Chant

Listening is the foundational skill of music.

We are not born able to understand and speak our mother tongue, but we learn it through consistent exposure and consistent practice. Likewise, we are not born able to understand and produce music, but it is learned in the same way as language: through consistent exposure and consistent practice.

Exposure is key. In the case of a child born deaf, they will never speak their language clearly, despite all the practice in the world, because spoken language is first acquired aurally (i.e. by listening), understood internally, and only then produced accurately with the voice. Music is like language in this way; it requires a foundation of listening and understanding.

Some instrumentalists get a kind of shortcut to making music, because their instrument, e.g. a piano, does much of the hard work of playing in tune and sounding good. Chanters get no such "free lunch". There is no shortcut to sounding good, because everything hinges on our aural skills from day one. Our instrument is not just our voice, but our voice and our ear working together. We can only be as good at chanting as we are at hearing.

Thus beginning chanting can feel slow and confusing. This is normal. We are building new skills from the ground up. Nobody should despair their musical inability any more than they despair their inability to speak some foreign language. Everyone begins with very little skill, if any. Everyone who practices consistently will improve.

To succeed we must employ the "Three P's:"

  • Prayer
  • Practice
  • Perseverance
And when things feel hard, one might add a fourth, Patience; and a fifth, self comPassion. Struggle is normal, and necessary for progress.

Byzantine Ear Training

The process employed on this site is known as "ear training", which is the process of developing aural skills and musical intuition through the use of structured exercises. As the title "Aural Foundations" suggests, these exercises are not "chanting" as such, but are a good way to train skills, such as singing in tune with a drone and recognising notes in a scale, which are essential and foundational skills for everyone who wants to chant.

Most ear training programs online use western theory, including chords (major, minor, etc.) and western interval names, which are largely irrelevant for students of Byzantine chant. Here the exercises are designed for chanters, and use the Byzantine parallagi:

Νη Πα Βου Γα Δι Κε Ζω Νη

Ni Pa Vou Ga Dhi Ke Zo Ni

Authentic Chanting relies on Tradition

A complete ear training program for Byzantine chanters must include lots of listening to recordings of skilled traditional chanters. Byzantine chant is an oral (as in "spoken") tradition, passed down through the life of the Orthodox church, and there is much that can only be learned by absorbing authentic chanting. The exercises on this site cannot fill this role. Here are just a few good traditional chanters you can listen to:

  • Panagiotis Neochoritis (Παναγιώτης Νεοχωρίτης)
  • Dimosthenis Paikopoulos (Δημοσθένης Παϊκόπουλος)
  • Ioannis Liakos (Ιωάννης Λιάκος)
